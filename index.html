Comparing Observed Meteorological Fields to Self-Organized Maps, or Neural Network Nodes

This page will display comparisons of observed meterological fields and observed precipitation fields to the Self-Organized Maps that they have been assigned to. I will start out with some of the wettest 6 hour periods observed in 2022. Upper level meteorological fields were generated in Python using data from the NCAR Reanalysis dataset. Surface precipitation images were also generated in Python, using data collected from the National Weather Service (NWS) Real-Time Mesoscale Analysis (RTMA) database.

The above table showed the ten SOM nodes that had the closest matching observed upper air patterns in 2023. The five digit number under each of the observed patterns is the sum of the 1035 point differnces between the SOM node array and the observed pattern array, squared. Dividing those numbers by 1035 and taking the square root of that number results in the root-mean square (RMS) difference of the two fields. Values around 16,000 mean the RMS difference is about 4 meters per point. Values around 25,000 correspond to RMS values about 5 meters per point. Even values around 36,000 are RMS differences of 6 meters per point, not all that different from 4 meters per point. However, over the course of the year 2023, the median RMS value was 7.5 meters per point, and there were about 100 patterns with RMS values larger than 10.

And now, this page has grown to be a little unwieldy. These images have shown that, at times, the SOM nodes show strong agreement with the observed field they are paired with. This agreement is generally stronger as the Root-Mean-Square (RMS) difference decreases. But sometimes the similarity of patterns extends, to larger RMS values as well. 500 hPa Height fields typically have values ranging from 5000 meters at northern latitudes and 6000 meters over the tropics. In this study, I normalized the height fields by subtracting the height of the middle point of the array from the rest of the values of the array. This was done to remove seasonal signals from the data. After subtracting the value of the center array point, the range of values across each height field was from about -100 meters up to about +50 meters. In the two years we looked at, 2022 and 2023, the RMS ranged from 2.04 meters up to 13.45 meters. The largest RMS value (weakest match between an observed field and its SOM node) was for a SOM node that ranged from -50 to +20 and an observed height field ranging from 4800 meters up to 6000 meters. Therefore, the RMS value was only about 20 percent of the range of that particular SOM node and is only one percent of the range of the observed height field. Interestingly, a qualitative comparison of these two images shows that the two PATTERNS are very similar, with a high amplitude ridge over the northeast Pacific, an upper trough over the northwestern U.S. and Canada and more zonal flow over the eastern United States.

RMS differences were a little higher for the 300 hPa Isotachs, ranging from a low of 6 knots up to a maximum of 16 knots. But the 16 knot RMS value was for an isotach field that ranged up to 90 knots, so the RMS value was only about 15 percent of the magnitude of the field being measured.

This data shows there is a limit to the RMS differences between the SOM nodes and observed fields. Even though some of the RMS values are relatively high, since the study is showing the minimum of values when compared to the other 1034 nodes, there are certainly nodes that match worse than the designated node. And now that a neural network of weather patterns has been defined, these SOM nodes can be used to compare observed weather pattterns across years and decades.
