Observed Meteorological Fields Compared to Self-Organized Maps

This page will display comparisons of observed meterological fields and observed precipitation fields to the Self-Organized Maps that they have been assigned to. I will start out with some of the wettest 6 hour periods observed in 2022. Upper level meteorological fields were generated in Python using data from the NCAR Reanalysis dataset. Surface precipitation images were also generated in Python, using data collected from the National Weather Service (NWS) Real-Time Mesoscale Analysis (RTMA) database.

The above table showed the ten SOM nodes that had the closest matching observed upper air patterns in 2023. The five digit number under each of the observed patterns is the sum of the 1035 point differnces between the SOM node array and the observed pattern array, squared. Dividing those numbers by 1035 and taking the square root of that number results in the root-mean square (RMS) difference of the two fields. Values around 16,000 mean the RMS difference is about 4 meters per point. Values around 25,000 correspond to RMS values about 5 meters per point. Even values around 36,000 are RMS differences of 6 meters per point, not all that different from 4 meters per point. However, over the course of the year 2023, the median RMS value was 7.5 meters per point, and there were about 100 patterns with RMS values larger than 10.
